My Reading Year – 2020

In 2019 I set a goal for myself: I would read a book a week (on average) and stop paying attention to all the social media distractions. For 2020, I kept that goal, and again I achieved it — and more.

Below are the books I read, in the order I read them, with a brief note on each. The same rule applied: I did not have to finish a book I wasn't enjoying -- life is far too short. So, assume that a book on this list has my endorsement as a "must-read" (assuming you plan to read a shit-ton of books this year).

One thing that will become clear is that, at a point, I start reading more books on anti-racism and the history of systemic discrimnation in the United States. Ijeoma Oluo, in Mediocre: The Dangerous Legacy of White Male America, says:

Almost every day I get a message from a white person saying, “Tell me what to do and I’ll do it.”

So not wanting to be that person, I set out to enducate myself. And that is reflected in my reading choices this year.

There are probably some spoilers, so apologies in advance.


The Instructions
By Adam Levin

A 10-year-old probable Messiah navigates first love in suburban Chicago. As one does. Over four days basically everything happens, and at over 1,000 pages, there's plenty of room for everything to happen. Gurion ben-Judah Maccabee is more precocious than all of Salinger's Glass siblings put together. The many pages devoted to how to go about engaging in your first kiss is alone worth the price of admission. (1026 pages)

Katrina: After The Flood
By Gary Rivlin

I read this as a primer before going on my first trip to New Orleans. The book reads something like the less interesting seasons of The Wire, but even then, like The Wire, it's pretty interesting. The amount of corruption in the months and years after the human made disaster that was the flooding of the city is disheartening. But it's punctuated by hope as the city claws its way back with the help of some who are not on the take. (480 pages)

The Nickel Boys
By Colson Whitehead

An exploration of the casual, capricious, and vicious racism of the 50s. Less visceral than "The Underground Railroad" but still a heartbreaking exploration of institutional brutality... (210 pages)

Lethal White
By Robert Galbraith

The fourth in the Cormoran Strike series has lost most of the sensational gore of it's predecessor. That seems to be in favor of developing the characters of Strike and his partner Robin and their relationships to each other and those around them. The mystery is fine, but ultimately the stakes aren't very high. This, plus J.K. Rowling's trans-exclusionism, will make this the last I read of our crusty detective and his interpid sidekick. (657 pages)

In The Dream House
By Carmen Maria Machado

In queer culture, the incidents of domestic violence are kept quiet. This memoir covers Machado's relationship with her partner who has a volatile temper and ability to use her charisma to inflict psychological abuse. This is not an easy book to read, but for me it shows a dynamic I really don't know much about. I am grateful for the author's ability to tell this story. (272 pages)

The City & The City
By China Mieville

There's a murder, sure, and an international mystery around who dunnit and why. But how do you investigate a murder in a city that is really two cities superimposed in time and space but where the residents of one city are not allowed to acknowledge the other city living right among them. Tyador Borlu, a senior inspector in the downtrodden Eastern European version of the city attempts to get to the bottom of it. (332 pages)

Night Boat To Tangier
By Kevin Barry

There is a menacing "Waiting For Godot" quality as two old friends spend the night in a ferry terminal looking for an erstwhile daughter. Old grievances, capers, loves, and hates form the backdrop of the story. How do we step out of the old patterns that seem to have us trapped? Can we? (258 pages)

I'm Thinking Of Ending Things
By Iain Reid

A thriller which set me on edge. It's creepy, and the line between horror and psychology is unclear until the end. I can't really say more than that so as not to spoil it. It has the flavor of a Shirley Jackson novel like "The Haunting of Hill House". (241 pages)

Use Of Weapons
By Iain Banks

Ruminations on the futility of violence, with a ton of violence. The third book in Banks's Culture series. This one features an interesting structure with two interweaved storylines, one moving into the past and the other moving into the future. When they come together, the result are...confusing? It's definitely worth reading, but be ready for some ambiguity. I'm not sure if it's due to Banks's being smarter then I am or if he just sort of fucks it up. (516 pages)

The Grammarians
By Cathleen Schine

Where does one twin end the other other begin? When they share a secret language, when they know what the other is thinking. When they can pull off the old switcheroo? When they lead parallel lives, including having a double wedding ceremony? They find their differences in the petty arguments around concepts of language and literature. One is a language scold who writes a syndicated column while the other uses historical passages from regular vernacular to assemble poetry. (272 pages)

Resilient Management
By Lara Hogan

The author is a veteran manager of people. Want to build teams that are highly productive? (105 pages)

True Grit
By Charles Portis

Mattie Ross is only fourteen years old, but she has grit -- you might even say she has true grit. The story of Mattie, Rooster Cogburn, and La Boeuf on the tail of the murderer Tom Chaney into the Indian Territory (now Oklahoma) is an adventure tale, but there's more here. The undercurrent of resentment among Southerners in the wake of the Civil War is almost another character in this book. Portis's tone seems mostly sympathetic to that resentment without deifying the "lost cause" narrative that gave rise to Jim Crow. Published in 1968, Rooster Cogburn is an early anti-hero, a forefather of Tony Soprano or Omar Little. He's a brutal killer who doesn't descriminate between the guilty or the innocent; he's massacred abolitionist towns and participated in range wars on the wrong side. It's hard to reconcile what we know about Rooster with our tendency to root for him to prevail. (216 pages)

The Bear
By Andrew Krivak

The Bear has gotten classified as a post-Apocalyptic novel, and I suppose that is the case, however, that's not particularly important in this tale. It's a story of a father's love for his daughter, his teaching her to live in harmony with nature, and her taking those lessons to their logical conclusion. This is as much a fable as anything with lessons to give about how to be in a world that is hard but not altogether uncaring. The author wrote about being influenced by Cormac McCarthy's All The Pretty Horses, and you will likely also see other shades of McCarthy. The writing is spare and clear with a cadence that sometimes feels like a walk in the woods interspersed with bursts of activity. Time moves both very quickly and very slowly. (224 pages)

The Institute
By Stephen King

I mean...it's Stephen King. Kids are being exploited by evildoers. The word "jeepers" appears twice! (577 pages)

The Hidden Girl and Other Stories
By Ken Liu

If you are very, very into the Singularity, this collection is for you. Most of the stories explore themes of human concisiousness melded with technology in some for or another. There's a bit of Von Neumann machine fiction and, strangely, a digression into fantasy about two-thirds of the way through the book. Many of the stories build and play on each other which is good when you like the stories they're building on and less great when you're not as into the underlying storyline. This collection drove home why I don't love short story collections: you aren't quite sure where the story is going to end and sometimes it ends far before you wish it would. (432 pages)

Frankissstein
By Jeanette Winterson

Three different first-person narratives weave through this novel exploring what it means to be human, to have an identity, and to have a soul. At the center is Mary Shelley, author of "Frankenstein," and to describe much more would be to divulge spoilers. The story switches between 19th Century London and modern-day Manchester and Arizona where...? (352 pages)

The Mirror & The Light
By Hillary Mantel

For two installments of this trilogy we watched Thomas Cromwell work his way into Henry VIII's most inner circle. He's avoided all the traps set for him while laying and springing his own. I can say one thing, I'd be terrible as a close advisor to a monarch, even one who fancies himself as enlightened as Henry does in these novels. It's strange to read a novel where you are so certain of the outcome. You know Thomas will not make it out alive, but I still found myself pulling for him right to the end. Of course, he deserves everything he gets and then some, but still -- he is our hero and we've come a long way since Stepney. (764 pages)

Station Eleven
By Emily St. John Mandel

I reread this in honor of the coronavirus lockdown. The interlocking storylines of people in the throws or aftermath of a fatal global pandemic makes it all seem very exciting and action-packed. Which is a nice distraction since our own pandemic experience, for the lucky uninfected, is a blur of days melding into other days. The Traveling Symphony at least gets to go outside. (354 pages)

The City We Became
By N. K. Jemison

When I read the short story this novel is based on last year, I was hoping there'd be more, and here it is. Jemison is a great storyteller, and the MacArthur Foundation seems to agree. New York City is much more than it appears -- it is, in fact, a living entity and shit is about to get weird. This is the first book in a series, and I hope Staten Island get a redemption storyline, because fuck Staten Island. (449 pages)

Heaven And Hell: A History of the Afterlife
By Bart Ehrman

Why is there Hell? How about Heaven? You won't find the concepts in the preaching of a scripture thumping hack from Galilee or his earliest lackeys. How'd we get to a lake of fire where bad people spend eternity as pentalty for a human lifetime of sin? 80ish years of greed or covetousness or licentiousness gets you a trillion trillion years of demonic torment? I try to read one or two Ehrman books per year because they make me think of things that my heathen ass rarely considers. (346 pages)

In A Lonely Place
By Barbara Hughes

How much more noir could it be? None, none more noir. It's 1947 Los Angeles and you are looking through the eyes of a serial killer. That's not a spoiler, we know all about Dixon Steele (?!) from about page 2. Barbara Hughes turns the noir genre on its head, and this book seems very fresh. (225 pages)

The Revisionaries
By A. R. Moxon

In the 80s we called this type of novel "postmodern," but those novels were usually nowhere near this enjoyable. As we start out it's only sort of wacky and off-kilter but it all gets more omnious then philosophical and meta. It's rare that I laugh aloud at a book, but this one caught me at it a few times. And the wordplay is sometimes goofy and then other times feels like it came out of my own mind. How many times have I wondered about "donut" versus "doughnut"? A lot, trust me on that. Solipsisim of the author. Free will of the fictional character. (600 pages)

The Factory
By Hiroko Oyamada

It's bleak and Kafaka-esque in this Japan. What is with these birds? This is a trippy trip through the lives of three people who work at the factory. But what is the factory? What does it do? Does it make anything? So much attention is paid to food in this story. And time doesn't hae much meaning as we seem to move around freely. (128 pages)

A Children's Bible: A Novel
By Lydia Millet

Both a novel and a call to action on climate change, the allegory will slap you in the face. If you have a passing knowledge of The Holy Bible, you'll recognize the players. There's Moses, washed up in a flooding lake, there's a parting of the Red Sea, a burning bush, and a literal ex-machina Deus ex-machina. This thing has it all including a crucifixion. It's not a bad story, it is just distracting to keep noticing the biblical stuff popping up all the time. (229 pages)

Don't Let's Go To The Dogs Tonight
By Alexandra Fuller

What is it like to be a young white girl in a majority black region undergoing revolutionary change? The author recounts here family's peripatetic life as farmers and ranchers in Kenya, Mozambique, and Zambia. At times the casual racism of the author and those around here is alarming, but we have the sense that while the author is looking fondly at these times, she is well aware of the problematic nature of what is going on. Her father, a farmer, is often gone fighting for the white regime in what was then Rhodesia. Her mother is shown to be a strong woman who keeps the faimly together, provides medical services to the local non-white people, and taught their children. They are both alcoholics steeped in the tragedy of several dead children, one of whom haunts the family as if he were an actual ghoast. (336 pages)

The Last Day
By Andrew Hunter Murray

Debut novel, could use a copy editor. Overall a decent thriller (379 pages)

Bloodchild: And Other Stories
By Octavia E. Butler

I very much regret that I only came to read Octavia Butler at the age of 55. I guess it's because she didn't fall within the sci-fi "canon" that I read. And that's just a bunch of sexism and racism. My fault entirely for not stepping out of those old white men circles. As I have started reading more scient fiction and fantasy again, I"m trying to expand those horizons and I'm glad for it. These stories engage in different ways - some aren't even scient fiction or fantastic, "Near Of Kin" is just a beautiful, small story that tells a simple tale with such empathy. I wish most of these were longer. (215 pages)

Giovanni's Room
By James Baldwin

David is the central player in this tight story of a man trying to figure out his own sexuality in a period of American conformity. Giovanni is a foil, and that's too bad as he is the more interesting character. We get very little insight into what makes Giovanni, the gay bartender, tick. We more easily understand David as he tries to pull away from a relationship with a woman but falls back into it out of sheer passivity. He's a young man -- in his late 20s -- floating through Europe trying to "find himself" but not really embrassing what he finds. (178 pages)

Office Clemmons: A Memoir
By Francois S. Clemmons

I was looking foward to reading this memoir François Clemmons who played Office Clemmons on Mister Rogers' Neighborhood. As a black man, I know he'd wrestled with playing the part of a police officer, and I wanted to know more about that in the context of the civil unrest of the last 60s and early 70s. Clemmons is a gay man whom Fred Rogers has asked to remain in the closet; I wanted to dig more deeply into their unique relationship.I wanted to learn more about the iconic photo on the book cover showing François and Mr. Rogers sharing a dip in a swimming pool at a time that was not common. Unfortunately, this memoir, is mostly a chronological ticking off of events in Clemmons' life. A memoir should be able to peel back the layers and get at the meet of a person's experience, but that doesn't happen in this book . A memoir doesn't have to be chronological -- I think of Let's Not Go To the Dogs Tonight as an example. In this memoir, there are three lackluster paragraphs on how the Harlem Spiritual Ensemble got its first agent, but there is no deep exploration of why that ensemble was formed. At no time does Clemmons talk about the photo on the cover of the book and what it meant to share that pool and have Fred share his towel to dry off. What happens with François and Nicky, who share a deep, years-long love that never gets explored and seems to disappear. François gets married in the 70s while not being at all interested in sex with his wife and living a side life on the down low, but he never explores that and the impact it had on the woman he seemed to be deceiving. And finally, and maybe the most egregions glossing is of his half-sister, Lawanna, ten years his junior, who committed suicide in 1975. We get only a cursory coverage of her suicide and how François found her dead body in her apartment: he got a call saying she hadn't been heard from in awhile, he went to her apartment, broke down the door, found her stabbed to death at her own hand, wept, the end. That takes about two pages in its entirety and comes way at the end of the book for some reason. He never explores what effect that had on him. Mr. Clemmons is, without doubt, an extremely accomplished artist. But I can't help feel that his story remains untold. (288 pages)

The Ballad of Songbirds And Snakes
By Suzanne Collins

The sollipsistic origin story that apparently explains Panem's broken society and future tyrant. Am I supposed to feel sympathy? (528 pages)

La Père Goriot (Old Man Goriot)
By Honoré de Balzac

This was my first Balzac novel and definitely not my last. As a big fan of Charles Dickens, I found the feel of this novel to be very inviting. The portrait of Paris both makes me want to be back in the Latin Quarter and also happy that I never had to deal with “society.” The story of young Rastignac’s rise and fall in society plays out against the Lear-like tragedy of old Goriot (288 pages)

White Fragility
By Robin DiAngelo

White people, like me, swim in a sea of white supremacy that allows us to go through life as “just people” without having to think about race or confront our place in the institutions which are pervaded by racism. But when our racial bubbles are pricked in any way we become defensive, hurt, condescending, argumentative, withdrawn, etc. This is white fragility. The author has faced plenty of criticism for her concept of white fragility, but as a white man at the pinnacle of a what supremacist hierarchy, this all rang true to me. Rarely did I think DiAngelo was off-base in her analysis. I have read responses to this book and the concept of white fragility, but none have really caused me to think this book is off-base. The frustrating take away, though, from this book is the feeling that getting out of this world of white supremacy is going to take more work than any one person can possibly do. (187 pages)

The Glass Hotel
By Emily St. John Mandel

Time, space, memory, and death are all malleable in this novel. A question comes up: Is it possible to both know something and not know that thing at the same time? (321 pages)

How To Be An Anti-Racist
By Ibram X. Kendi

I originally read this last year, before the current protests, and I decided I need a refresher. A second reading, after reading some other antiracist literature, has really opened up the meaning of this book for me. I understand it much better the second time through, and I see a clear path now for using what Kendi is talking about in my day-to-day life. Racism is a manifestation of racist policies and racist policies stem from radical self-interest. We can change racism by actively fighting to change racist policies. (284 pages)

The Captain and The Glory: An Entertainment
By Dave Eggers

This is a real "over the rainbow" portrayal of the Trump admistration and its handling of the country. To call this "An Entertainment" as Eggers does may be overstating it a bit, or maybe it's ironic. Either way, it's very difficult to be entertained at the expense of the idiocracy any longer. This treatment hits every point of cruelty and craven cluelessness we know too well. The main Trump flunkies are present, his weird relationship with his daughter gets weirder, Stephen Miller lives on as a spooky ghost in the machine that drives the worst impulses of The Captain. Did I like this little tail? Not really? (120 pages)

White Rage: The Unspoken Truth of Our Racial Divide
By Carol Anderson

Brings home the history of the systemic war white supremacists have been waging on black people since the end of the Civil War. We like to think it all ended the, but it clearly did not, and the supremacists has continually plagued the nation via political maneuvering, violence, deceit, etc. Many of us know that Reconstruction had the legs kicked out from under it, but the asautls on Brown v. Board and on the impediments to the Great Migration were less well known to me, and I've read about Thurgood Marshall's efforts as an NAACP lawyer. The more recent issues around voting rights are more familiar, but no less infuriating. (304 pages)

Love: A Novel
By Roddy Doyle

Davy and Joe are getting hammered, just like old times, as they pub-hop through Dublin. The book is that one long evening of Joe recounting leaving his wife for the woman of his dreams from 35 years before. Davy is in Dublin to care for his dying father. The story is simple, but the questions around what is memory and how does our past lived reality survive memory. What does it mean to be conscious in the moment? At one point Davy describes an episode he had as a dotted line. I was reminded a little of Vonnegut's Kilgore Trout, a little unstuck in time. Did Joe really even go to France with is dream woman? (335 pages)

Stamped From The Beginning
By Ibram X. Kendi

Kendi covers hundreds of years of racism in the United States -- actually before, going back to the early colonial period. Six rough "eras" are covered using an individual as an anchoring device and exploring the world of American racism swirling around them. This book is a great foundation for his "How To Be An Antiracist" as it covers in detail topics like "uplift suasion" and educatoin and why these techniques are doomed to fail due to the core cause of institutional racism being self-interest, not ignorance or hatred. As he formulates it, and persuasively carries throughout this work, the cause of racism as we know it is self-interest that led to policies that led to hate. It all seems quite dismal and one wonders how this can be overcome, but Kendi offers hope throughout the book and then brings it all together in the epilogue where he encourages us to lean into the self-interest of antiracism. (583 pages)

Interference
By Sue Burke

We're generations in the future of the planet Pax, and Earthlings are coming to see how their colony is faring. This is the same colony settled in Semiosis and the colony is doing well, living in harmony as two alien species who settled a planet populated with sentient plants. The alpha plant, so far as we know is Stevland, a rainbow bamboo with a yen to spread its seeds widely. The story if complex, and it sometimes seems to get away from the author. The action sequences tend to be confusing, but the story is so intriguing it's easy to overlook that. There is so much left unexplored, I have a hard time believing Sue Burke will be able to leave this as a "duaology" as the series title leads one to believe. (315 pages)

Their Eyes Were Watching God
By Zora Neale Hurston

Zora Neal Hurston is a legend of literature, and I'm embarrassed to admit that this is the first of her books that I have read. But I'm so glad I finally did. It was Ibram X. Kendi's Stamped From The Beginning that prompted me to finally read her, and I chose what is probably her most well-known book. The story spans the first third of the 20th Century in the American South which is clearly segrated but in which White people barely appear but whose effect is devastating. The story of Janie is framed as a converation with her friend, Pheobe. Janie is a black woman in the Jim Crow South that is also steeped in period misogyny. Janie is the epitome of intersectonal oppression: a Black person, a woman, light-skinned, "good" haired. At the center of those intersections, Janie is stoic, stolid, patient, and courageous. She kicks all the ass. What were they watching for as they watched God? It seems to be a deliverance that never comes and that one has to take for ones own. (216 pages)

The Great CEO Within
By Matt Mochary

Lots of hands-on advice for how to build a company. Some is truly only relevant to a startup CEO, but there are pieces that can be applied to larger companies and teams within those companies. The sections on personal efficiency reach behind the needs of the CEO. (196 pages)

Why Are All The Black Kids Sitting Together In The Cafeteria?: And Other Conversations About Race
By Beverly Daniel Tatum

Children start to build their racial identities early and efforts to rear "colorblind" children will fail when we refuse to acknowledge race. The author is a psychologist, and at times I found this book pretty academic, but it was punctuated by very insightful and helpful observations. Was it the most riveting book on race in American? No, it was not. But it was worth reading for another perspective on how we can address the systemic racism in this country. (505 pages)

This Book Is Anti-Racist
By Tiffany Jewell

I've been doing a lot of antiracism reading recently because......this, and this specific book is on my list because it is aimed at young people. I want to make sure my yound nieces and nephew get exposure to these issues, and this seemed a good book to suggest. So, I read it to make sure what is in it would be appropriate to kids aged 11 or so. Along with informaiton on the history of racism and the difference between inividual prejudice and instituional racism, there are several activities to get the reader to think about their own experiences with race and prejudice. (160 pages)

Utopia Avenue
By David Mitchell

Welcome to the universe of David Mitchell circa 1967-69 in Swingin' Sixties London. I'm a sucker for Mitchell, and this one did not disappoint. The story follows the formation of a new pop band and their minor rise and decline. This isn't "Behind The Music" though there is plenty of sex and drugs -- the band just isn't that successful. But there is real story and character here that kept me interested in what each member of Utopia Avenue is going through without the theatrics of the rock lifestyle. Rest assured, since you are in the world of David Mitchell, things do get pretty weird at one point. You probably should have read The Thousand Summers and The Bone Clocks before reading this or you may find yourself confused and you will definitely miss some inside references. (570 pages)

Wanderers
By Chuck Wendig

About 100 pages into this novel I was ready to give up and move on to something else. But the plot was relatively interesting, and it dawned on me that what I was really reading was a screenplay and not a novel. The story is derivative from classics like The Stand or more recent work on a similar topic like The Passage. My guess is the author is aiming for some type of limited run series on HBO with this one. Basically, it's and end-of-the-world novel with crazy religious overtones, racist death cults, the usual. None of the characters are very interesting and I had no investment in any of them. I guess that's up to the actors they cast to bring the pathos. The author is very, very, into similes. And not very good similies. So many bad similes. Also, the word sursurrus is used four times in the novel -- why? When you get to be a bigtime writer does Random House stop putting your work through any sort of copy editing? Coming on the heals of a David Mitchell novel this thing was a mess. (783 pages)

So You Want To Talk About Race
By Ijeomi Oluo

Over the course of this year I have been in several all-hands work meetings where issues of race in the workplace were discussed. We're talking hundreds of people. Usually the speakers are my Black colleagues. In every one of those gatherings, some White person will ask a variation on "tell me how I can be an ally." That question troubles me -- it's wrong to ask the target of system racism to explain it to those who benefit from that racism. We need to do some homework, my Whites. Ijeomi Oluo is taking on the emotional weight of explaining our systemic racism to us, so this is a good place to start in learning what our friends and co-workers experience. It's not the last word on this topic, but it's a good overview of things we should know. The author holds out hope for us: "People are afraid of getting these conversations wrong, but they are still trying, and I deeply appreciate that. These conversations will not be easy, but they will get easier over time." (266 pages)

The Vanishing Half
By Brit Bennett

On its face the "half" in the title seems to refer to one of a pair of twin sisters who disappears, going off to life her live passing as a white woman in America. But there are so many parts and halves represented in this novel. It seems as if every character has a corresponding half. Aside from Desiree and Stella, the twins, there are their respective daughters, the husband/partner they each have, a transgender man's half is the formerly female identity he's leaving behind. This is a tender story of lies told and maintained that play out just as lies do in life. There are many loose ends never tied up, consequences avoided, secrets left unexplored. This is only the author's second novel, but she takes such care in crafting her characters. As a reader, I always felt cared for. The small stories within the stories a profound in what they tell you about the people living these lives. (350 pages)

Begin Again: James Baldwin's America and Its Urgent Lessons for Our Own
By Eddie S. Glaude, Jr.

"There will be no moral appeals on my part to this country’s moral conscience. It has none." The author tells the story of Baldwin and his work through the lense of one "after time" and applied to another "after time" as a way of responding to Trumpism and the underlying lie of the american "value gap" that posits one group as more valuable than another based on skin color. There are some problems in that baldwin (and glaude) seem to want to remove categorizations that are at the crux of oppression in this country. It seems like they're wanting us to just look at people as people, but we know that is not how things really work. It seems naive. It also discounts other intersectionalities that are at play in the great lie. (274 pages)

The Biggest Bluff
By Maria Konnikova

Surprisingly stressful reading. (364 pages)

Axiom's End
By Lindsay Ellis

It's not a spoiler: several aliens have been living in a government facility for decades. Yet all attempts to communicate with them have failed. Til now. It's pretty typical government coverup conspiracy theory, with a "whistleblower" that seems to be modeled on Julian Assange, our heroine who is his estranged daughter and who happens to become best buds with an alien sent to find his wayward alien pals. Oh, and there's another less friendly alien crew sent to Earth as well. There's a lot going on, and not all of it is particularly entertaining. This is first of a series of novels, and I don't plan to continue on past this one. (371 pages)

White Negroes
By Lauren Michele Jackson

The author makes a compelling argument for how anything interesting in white culture has been appropriated from Black culture. "But what about...," you might be thinking -- I was. But then I gave it more thought, and it's a hard argument to rebut. While I'm not particularly interested in most of the bits of culture she focuses on (Xtina, crying Jordan memes, etc.) almost everything that is presented can be extrapolated to any cultural phenomemon in white culture. (199 pages)

Alaric The Goth
By Douglas Boin

The Roman Empire's fall has been told and described from a lot of perspectives. In this story, the protaganist is a Gothic warrior and leader named Alaric, a historic character who led a daring raid on the city of Rome. The author appears to do a lot of filling in of blanks in the historical record based on other contemporary accounts. There's probably also a fair amount of poetic license here, but I'm not equipped to call that out. It's an entertaining telling of a story with some points that are relevant for our own times. The author points out the dependence of the late Western Roman Empire on "foreigners" and the shoddy treatment they received by Roman citizens. Some of that treatment may have been related to why the empire ultimately fell. (269 pages)

Piranesi
By Susanna Clarke

Is this an allegory of some kind, a story of psychosis, a cautionary tail of megalomania, a murder mystery? Yep, it's all those things and probably much more. (243 pages)

The Enigma of Clarence Thomas
By Corey Robin

"[Thomas's] jurisprudence may be a bitter mix of right-wing revanchism and black nationalism, but it is distinctively American and of the moment. It begins with the belief that racism is permanent, the state is ineffective, and politics is feeble, and ends with a dystopia that looks painfully familiar: men armed to the teeth, people locked up in jails, money ruling all, and racial conflict as far as the eye can see." "If Thomas begins from premises that are shared yet arrives at conclusions from which we recoil, that might argue for closer scrutiny of the steps he takes along the way...[and] we may come to a realization: that the task at hand is not to retrace and rebut his moves from premise to conclusion, but to go back and start again with different premises." Ask me the things I remember about Clarene Thomas's confirmation hearing, and I would recall something Anita Hill said about a Coke can and a pubic hair and "Long Dong Silver." But Clarence Thomas himself didn't make any impression other than most definitely being NOT Thurgood Marshall. He was also definitely a perjurer, but that's par for the course in these hearings. Now, 30 years later, Thomas is the longest serving Justice with over 700 opinions authored, and I bet most people has no idea what drives him or what one can derive as driving him from his record. This book is not a biography, it's a chilling analysis of his jurisprudence around race, capitalism, and the constitution. I had a troubling feeling while reading this analysis as sometimes the rationale informing Thomas's opinions landed well with me, but the conclusions he drives to are abominable in their nihilistic paternalism. It would be very easy to be drawn in by his positions and you have to take a look around and realize that what you're reading is monstrous. If you follow Thomas, you have to stop believing our country can be a better place than it was in 1950. (303 pages)

News Of The World
By Paulette Jiles

A little bit of "True Grit," a little Cormac McCarthy lite. It's truly a Western road novel where our hero is contracted to return a young girl to her family, requiring him to travel from North Texas to South Texas in the years directly after the Civil War. The Indian Wars are in full swing, and there are dangers galore, but not from any indiginous people. If you like a Western adventure with some mildly eccentric characters, you will like this one. (210 pages)

Leave The World Behind
By Rumaan Alam

This one was getting a ton of buzz off the back of a Today Show recommendation, and I was hopeful. About halfway through I started to lose confidence in the story, but I stuck it out. It was so close to being a story I could get into, but ultimately it's a story about what happens when normal people are unprepared for the end of civilization. All the little slights that we ignore take on a new significance is the moral, I guess. I prefer my end-of-the world stories to be set after the actual boring parts of the internet failing, wildlife anomalies, omninously beautiful weather, running out of the good booze, friendly yet threatening and armed neighbors, etc. If we have to sit through all that, at least let there be zombies wandering the countryside. (253 pages)

How Much of These Hills Is Gold
By C. Pam Zhang

Two young children of Chinese parent, in 1864, on their own looking for the right place to bury their dead father. That's the setup that propels a story that is much deeper than that. Identity and the struggle of a person against their idea of themselves and the ideas others put upon them is central to this story. What does it mean to present an identity? What does it mean to have your identity assumed though it's not really yours? We think of these questions as very contemporary, but the author suggests this is an old situation that recurs and recurs. (280 pages)

Mexican Gothic
By Silvia Moreno-Garcia

Spooky, secluded house? Check! Creepy servants who don't speak? Yes! Lots and lots of mist? Of course! Nearby village with helpful yokels? Goes without saying! Voices, dreams, moving wallpaper? I feel like Stepon on Weekend Update. This story has it all! Every gothic trope has been whirred up in a Ninja and poured over 1950s Mexico. The main character is straight from an old movie; I was able to instantly visualize this slick, city-wise, flirty heroine who smokes, loves to drive and won't take no guff from any old Palooka. If this was really an old movie she'd be played by Kathryn Hepburn with slightly darkened skin. Lionel Barrymore as the racist old patriarch. Bogart as the menacing Palooka. If you're looking for a moderately scary story with all the gothic doodads, this is for you. (305 pages)

Tulsa, 1921: Reporting A Massacre
By Randy Krehbiel

The author is a journalist and uses that lense to deeply explore and explain the events around the Greenwood Massacre that took place on May 31, 1921 in Tulsa, Oklahoma. Krehbiel uses accounts from the two local, rival, newspapers to lay the groundwork of his account along with other contemporary sources. The story starts well before May 31, 1921 and continues to present day where the aftermath of the massacre is still being felt. The racism and jealousy of the local White population is front-and-center as the current running through the entire course of events that day. From an encounter between a porter, a black man, and an elevator operator, a white woman, which may not have even happened to the financial ruin of most of Greenwood's black leaders to the White newspapers rebranding of the massacre as a "race riot" and to the search for mass graves of those Black citizens killed -- the author covers 100 years of history that has been largely forgotten outside Tulsa. This isn't an easy report to read, and the names and places sometimes all start to sound the same. But it's a story that needs to be remembered. (329 pages)

Guns, Germs, And Steel: The Fates of Human Societies
By Jared Diamond

This was originally published in 1997, and I'm surprised it took me so long to get to it. I guess it's the size -- I recall seeing it on the tables at Borders Books and thinking it was really too thick. Also, in some sense, I got the drift just from the title. White Europeans possessed the items listed in the title, thus they won. Understood. But what I didn't really think too much about, and the entire point of this book is, what is the ultimate underlying reason they got these items? How did Spain, France, England, Holland, etc. come to dominate so much of the world? At the time China was a united burgeoning world power, Europe was a bunch of tribes stabbing each other with spears. Human life began in Africa and flourished in the Fertile Crescent of the Middle East -- why didn't Persia become to the dominating world power of the early 20th Century instead of Great Britain? Why didn't the Aztecs conquer Spain? Those are the questions Diamond is examining. The anwsers as he presents them are fascinating. This is a thick book, and sometimes it gets a bit repetitive, but it's worth reading to get beneath the world we see around and understand what got us here. (644 pages)

Everywhere You Don't Belong
By Gabriel Bump

I think I have a good sense of humor, so whenever I read a novel that reviewers have claimed is "sharply funny" or "comic," I wonder what I'm missing. Yes, sure, there are witty and quirky parts of this novel, and I enjoyed the book very much. I took it as an impressionistic look, sometimes almost poetry, of life on the South Side of Chicago. Claude is a nerdy loner wondering where he belongs, looking for friends and direction for his life. The author introduces great characters that weave throughout the man vignettes that drive the story. There's Claude's ever-present Grandma and her platonic companion Paul, both guiding the young Claude. Claude's parents are only in the story for a short time, but their influence drives Claude to life-changing revelations. There's Janice, possibly Claude's only constant, though she is herself not content with where she is in the world. Bubbly, Nugget, Chester Dexter, Renaissance, The Twins, Truck, Big Columbus, Simone, Connie Stove, The Redbelters, the white boys, Martin the taco artist, Juna and her father, and the list goes on. These are just some of the characters who pass through Claude's life -- many of whom could have their own complete novels. This is the sort of story that sticks with you. (264 pages)

Mediocre: The Dangerous Legacy of White Male America
By Ijeoma Oluo

Ijeoma Oluo's new book pulls no punches. The title alone is provocative. If you're a White heterosexual man in American (hey, that's me!) you're going to feel uncomfortable -- and that's a good thing! It can be difficult to read while a tiny voice in my head cries "but, but, but" even while I'm nodding my head in agreement with the author's point. A key point is that White men, even the ones we like and think are "good people" are ardent defenders of mediocre positions that protect their own status quo. Joe Biden? Mediocre. Bernie Sanders? Yep. Higher education as formulated and carried out by White man institutions? Yeah, mediocre. ( pages)
